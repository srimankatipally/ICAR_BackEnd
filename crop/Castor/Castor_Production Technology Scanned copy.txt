PREFACE
Castor (Ricinus communis L.) provides an important industrial oil, which
is completely biodegradable. Castor oil is used for deriving a number of industrial
products viz., dye, detergents, plastics, printing ink, patent leather, ointment, polishes,
surface paints, adhesives, lubricants and hydraulic fluids. Castor cake is considered
to be an excellent organic manure. In eri-silk producing areas, castor leaves are fed
to eri-silk worms. India holds a premier and dominant position in the world in castor
production and supplies. During 2018-19, castor crop was grown in an area of 7.61 lakh
hectares, with 12.15 lakh tonnes production and a productivity of 1597 kg/ha. Despite
phenomenal increase in the production and productivity over the last two decades, there
are wide regional disparities in the productivity of castor. With the exception of Gujarat
and Rajasthan, where the productivity has registered a significant jump since 1970,
the general productivity level of rainfed castor in all other parts of the country is very
low. A multitude of factors such as its cultivation in sub-marginal and marginal lands,
uncertainty of rainfall and its erratic distribution, delayed sowings, poor management
of the crop with little or no inputs and use of poor quality seed are responsible for low
yields under rainfed situations.
This publication entitled Castor: Production Technology is a compilation
of concerted research efforts made at ICAR-Indian Institute of Oilseeds Research and
All India Coordinated Research Project on Oilseeds (castor), which has resulted in the
development of improved hybrids and varieties; improved management practices and
protection technologies capable of boosting the productivity in different agro-ecological
conditions in the country.

Introduction
Castor (Ricinus communis L.) is one of the ancient and important non-edible
oilseeds having immense industrial and medicinal value. It is an ideal candidate
for production of high value industrial and oil feed-stocks because of the very
high oil content (48-56%) in seed. The plant has unique ability to produce
oils with extremely high levels (80-90%) of ricinoleic acid. The potential seed
yield and unique fatty acid composition of castor, allows this oil to provide
economically, competitive feed-stocks needed for the production of premium
quality biodiesel, short chain aviation fuels, fuel lubrication additives and
very high value biopolymers. Castor oil has varied and versatile uses. Castor
oil and its products are widely utilized in manufacturing of nylon fibres, jet-
engine lubricants, hydraulic fluids and other products. The oil and its products
are widely used in preparation of toilet soaps, shaving cream, nail polish,
shampoo, sun cream, hair conditioner, antiperspirant and talcum powders. Not
only the seed and its products, but castor leaves, roots, flowers and stalks have
various curative properties and other benefits. Castor cake is considered to be
an excellent organic manure. In eri-silk producing areas, castor leaves are fed
to eri-silk worms.
India holds a premier and dominant position in the world in castor
production and supplies. During 2018-19, castor crop was grown in an area
of 7.61 lakh hectares, with 12.15 lakh tonnes production and a productivity of
1597 kg/ha. Despite phenomenal increase in the production and productivity
over the last two decades, there are wide regional disparities in the productivity
of castor. With the exception of Gujarat and Rajasthan, where the productivity
has registered a significant jump since 1970, the general productivity level
of rainfed castor in all other parts of the country is very low. A multitude of
factors such as its cultivation in sub-marginal and marginal lands, uncertainty
of rainfall and its erratic distribution, delayed sowings, poor management of
the crop with little or no inputs and use of poor quality seed are responsible
for low yields under rainfed situations. However, concerted research efforts
made at ICAR-Indian Institute of Oilseeds Research and under the All India
Coordinated Research Project on Oilseeds (castor) have resulted in the
development of improved hybrids and varieties coupled with production
and protection technologies capable of boosting the productivity in different
agro-ecological conditions in the country. The improved production practices
recommended for different castor growing areas in the country are presented
in this bulletin.
1
Areas of cultivation
Gujarat is the leading state in area (5.22 lakh ha), production (9.57 lakh tonnes)
and 1834 kg/ha productivity followed by Rajasthan, Telangana and Andhra
Pradesh. Gujarat contribute about 63 per cent of the area and 78 per cent of
the total castor production of the country. The other producing states are Tamil
Nadu, Karnataka, Odisha, Maharashtra and Haryana. The following states
have potential to produce castor viz., Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Bihar
and Jharkhand.
Production constraints
The constraints for castor production in rainfed areas are:
Low and erratic distribution of rainfall leading to drought
Delayed monsoon leading to late sowings in Telangana, Andhra Pradesh,
Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra and Odisha
Marginal and sub-marginal lands having shallow depth and poor fertility
Poor quality seed of varieties/hybrids
Low input use by the resource-poor farmers
Lack of seed production of hybrids with low seed replacement ratio (SRR)
Occurrence of diseases like wilt, Botrytis and insect pests viz., capsule
borer, tobacco caterpillar, semilooper.
The constraints for castor production in irrigated areas are:
Salinization of soils
Wilt and root rot problems due to continuous cropping
Incidence of semilooper in early stages
Non-availability of quality seeds of hybrids
Menace of blue bull in Gujarat, Rajasthan and Haryana
Climate
Castor is tolerant to drought and grows well in relatively dry and warm regions
having a well distributed rainfall of 500-750 mm. In heavy rainfall areas,
the crop puts on excessive vegetative growth and assumes a perennial habit.
2
The crop can grow even at an altitudes of 1200 to 2100 m and the perennial
varieties are grown even at higher altitudes for shade in coffee plantations.
Castor requires a moderate temperature (20-26°C) with low humidity (RH
60%) throughout the growing season to produce maximum yields. In India,
the crop is usually raised during kharif. It is also cultivated as a rabi crop in
Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu and in some parts of southern India
with assured irrigation. The crop is also grown on the river banks in Odisha,
Madhya Pradesh and Uttar Pradesh. It can withstand long dry spells as well as
heavy rains, but is highly susceptible to waterlogging.
Soils
Castor requires well-drained soils. It is generally grown on red, sandy loams in
southern and peninsular India and on light alluvial soils in the north-western
states of Gujarat and Rajasthan. Castor can also be grown in well drained light
black soils. Soils that are not suitable for growing valuable commercial and
food crops are often used for raising castor crop.

Cropping systems
Intercropping
Castor+redgram (4:1 or 6:1) was the most predominant intercropping
system in Telangana, Andhra Pradesh and other southern states. For
realizing higher economic returns, Castor+redgram (1:1) should be
followed.
In North-Gujarat, relay intercropping of Castor+greengram (1:1); in
Saurashtra relay intercropping of Castor+soybean (1:2) were profitable.
In Tamil Nadu, Castor+groundnut (1:5); Castor+blackgram/greengram
(1:2) and Castor+small onion (1:2) were found profitable.
Castor+green pea and other vegetables were found profitable in Haryana.

Agronomic management
Tillage and seed bed preparation
In shallow soils, deep summer ploughing helps to break the hard soil pan
and facilitates easy root penetration and crop growth apart from controlling
weeds, insect pests and diseases. For ensuring proper tilth and good seed bed
preparation, plough the soil immediately after the receipt of pre-monsoon
showers. Perform 2-3 harrowings with blade harrow after rains.
Method of planting/soil and moisture conservation
Ridge and furrow method of land configuration is better than flatbed
method as it helps in moisture conservation and reduces the impact of dry
spell. Making conservation furrows (dead furrow) at 2.4 to 3.6 m interval
after 45 days after sowing also conserves soil moisture.
In rainfed areas with undulated topography, proper preparation of land,
sowing the crop across the slope, 2-3 intercultures and strengthening of
ridges, along the rows after the last interculture (40 days after sowing)
helps in conservation of soil moisture apart from controlling weeds,
thereby resulting in increased seed yield.
Seeding time
Castor is predominantly grown during kharif season.
The suggested optimum seeding time for rainfed castor is second fortnight
of June in Telangana and Andhra Pradesh and first fortnight of July in
Gujarat and Rajasthan.
It can also be raised during rabi under irrigated conditions. The yield of
irrigated rabi crop was found to be higher than that of kharif crop, due to
relatively low incidence of insect pests, diseases and weeds.
7
Planting rabi castor during first fortnight of October was found to be ideal,
while January is optimum for sowing summer castor.
In case the other kharif crops could not be sown due to delayed monsoon,
castor can form a better contingent crop for sowing even up to 15th August
in rainfed areas of Telangana and Andhra Pradesh.
Quality seed/source
Farmers are advised to procure good quality viable seeds of hybrid/variety
from authorized/registered seed agent.
Farmers need to renew seed stock of hybrids every year and improved
varieties once in 4-5 years.
Pre-soaking and seed treatment
For quick germination and better stand establishment in dryland conditions,
soak the seed in fresh water (1:1 W/V) for about 10 hours to eliminate
chaffy seed and dry under shade.
Seeds should be treated with thiram or captan @ 3 g/kg seed or carbendazim
2 g/kg to protect plants from seed borne diseases like Alternaria leaf
blight, seedling blight and wilt.
Alternately, treat the seed with Trichoderma @ 10 g/kg seed and soil
application @ 2.5 kg incubated in 125 kg FYM/ha for managing wilt.
Seed rate
A seed rate of 5 kg/ha for hybrids and about 8-10 kg/ha for varieties is
recommended.

Fertilizer application
Castor responds well to nutrient application and produce higher yields.
Nearly 5 t/ha of farm yard manure or 1.25 to 1.5 t/ha of vermicompost
needs to be applied and incorporated into the soil, well in advance to
sowing for moisture conservation and nutrition.
Nutrient recommendation must be based on soil test value which helps in
avoiding excess fertilizer use thus reduces expenditure. Most of the castor
growing soils in different states are mostly low in nitrogen, medium to
high in phosphorus and potash.

For rainfed castor, apart from application of basal dose of 20 kg N
fertilizer, top dressing with 20 kg N/ha each at 35-40 and 65-70 days
after sowing depending upon the moisture availability in the soil provides
reasonable economic returns. In the event of a dry spell during 50-55 days,
first top dressing with 20 kg N/ha has to be done at the time of initiation
of secondaries with the receipt of rain.
10
For irrigated castor in Gujarat, nitrogen has to be given in 5 splits, 40 kg/
ha each at sowing, 40, 70, 100 and 130 days after sowing. In soils deficient
in Zn (if the soil available Zn is < 1.2 ppm) and Fe (if the soil available
Fe is < 3.7 ppm for non calcareous soil and < 6.3 ppm for calcareous
soil), 10 kg Zn SO₄ and 30 kg Fe SO₄/ha, respectively has to be applied to
obtain enhanced yields. For irrigated castor in Rajasthan, nitrogen should
be applied in 3 splits, 40 kg N as basal and 20 kg N/ha each at 35 and 80
days after sowing.
For integrated nutrient management in irrigated castor in Rajasthan,
application of 20 kg N through castor cake, 20 kg N through FYM, 20
kg N through in situ green manuring together with 20 kg N/ha through
inorganic fertilizer has been recommended. For irrigated castor in North
Gujarat and Saurashtra region of Gujarat, it has been recommended to
apply 30 kg N in the form of FYM, 30 kg N through castor cake, 30 kg
N through green manure and 30 kg N/ha through inorganic fertilizer for
reaping higher yields.
In light soils of Gujarat, seed treatment with Azospirillum or phosphate
solubilizing bacteria (PSB) @ 50 g/kg seed, apart from application of 75-
50-0 N: P₂O₅: K2O kg/ha in conjunction with castor cake (1 t/ha) or FYM
(5 t/ha) is advocated.
In Saurashtra region of Gujarat, it has been recommended to apply 20 kg
S/ha through gypsum or single super phosphate for realizing higher yields
and returns.
All the fertilizers have to be applied by pocketing method or in the furrow
made with the help of plough near by the plant and followed by covering
with soil, rather than broadcasting method or band placement. It helps
in efficient utilization of applied nutrients by the crop, besides avoiding
fertilizer wastage and weed growth.
Seed treatment with phosphate solubilising fungi, biophos (Chaetomium
globosum) @ 30 g inoculants/50 g seed or PSB (Phosphate solubilising
bacteria) before sowing along with application of 20 kg P₂O₅/ha as basal is
sufficient in case of low to medium P soils. It saves half of the expenditure
on P fertilizer without affecting seed yield of castor. In South Saurashtra
agro-climatic zone it is recommended to apply 40 kg P₂O₅/ha along with
biophos seed treatment for realizing higher seed yield and net returns.
Integrated nutrient management which envisages conjunctive use of
organic, inorganic and biological sources of nutrients for sustaining the
11
soil health and crop productivity, application of 50% RDF (recommended
dose of fertilizer)+seed treatment with Azospirillum+25% N through
FYM is advocated for castor under rainfed conditions.
In case of Zn deficit soils (<0.6 ppm), the most visible deficiency symptoms
are short internodes and a decrease in leaf size, delayed maturity and
improper seed filling. To correct this, 0.5% ZnSO₄ at 50 and 90 DAS (days
after sowing) has to be sprayed which helps in better growth and seed
filling besides reducing sucking pest load.
Under normal rainfall distribution, application of FYM along with
100% or 50% N: P₂O₅: K2O resulted in highest seed yield of sorghum
- castor cropping system in Alfisol under rainfed conditions. Under
drought situation, application of 25% N through FYM along with 75%
N: P₂O₅: K2O as inorganic fertilizer realized significantly highest mean
seed yield of sorghum (3117kg/ha) and castor (910 kg/ha). The INM
practice also resulted in higher organic C (0.5%) and available N (210
kg/ha) with higher moisture content in organic treatments. Substituting
25% N requirement through FYM could mitigate the effects of drought
effectively with sustainable soil fertility.
Irrigation
Castor crop needs 500-600 mm water for good growth and development.
Castor is highly sensitive to excess moisture; hence, care should be taken
to avoid waterlogging at any stage. Sufficient drainage or slope has to be
provided to drain out excess water from the field.
During kharif season, castor is mostly grown as rainfed crop. However, in
case of prolonged dry spells during grand growth period, one protective
irrigation either at primary spike development or secondary spike initiation/
development will greatly benefit the crop and improves the yield.
Under intensive management, all currently recommended popular,
medium, long duration hybrids/varieties require 5-7 irrigations in sandy
loams of Gujarat and Rajasthan and six in the red soils of Telangana and
Andhra Pradesh. For realizing the full productivity potentials, the first
irrigation should be applied at around 55-75 days or around full flowering
of primary spike. The subsequent irrigations may be given at intervals
of 20 days after first irrigation, so as to ensure availability of adequate
moisture for the development of different spike orders.
12
For rabi castor, irrigation has to be given after sowing in a dry soil. After
establishment of crop with optimum plant population, irrigation has to be
scheduled at IW/CPE ratio of 0.6 to 1.0 which means 12-15 days interval
from October to December and 8 days interval from January onwards, for
reaping higher yields.
It is also always better to give irrigation by ridge and furrow method rather
than flat bed method, in order to reduce water wastage.
In the regions of water shortage with meager irrigation water availability,
drip irrigation has to be scheduled at 0.8 pan evaporation i.e. for 1.5 to
2.0 hours from October to December and 2.5 to 3.0 hours from January
onwards at an interval of three days.
Planting of rabi castor (DCH-519) during 1st week of October and basal
soil application of 40 kg P₂O₅ through SSP; 80 kg N in the form of urea and
30 kg K2O in the form of sulphate of potash applied in equal splits through
drip-fertigation at 0.8 Epan at an interval of 10 days realized higher seed
yield (3302 kg/ha), oil yield (1479 kg/ha) with high profitability of rabi
castor with saving of 27% water in Alfisols of Telangana and Andhra
Pradesh.
In South Saurashtra Agro-climatic Zone it is recommended to irrigate the
crop at 0.8 PE through drip irrigation and apply nitrogen @ 90 kg/ha (20
kg N/ha as a basal and remaining 70 kg N/ha through drip in the form
of urea in five equal splits an interval of 12 days starting after cessation
of monsoon) along with recommended dose of phosphorus (50 kg/ha) as
basal for realizing higher seed yield and net returns.
Drip irrigation helps to save nearly 20-25% of irrigation water. At the
same, water use efficiency can be doubled as compared to flood irrigation.
Weeding and interculture
Weed menace is moderate to severe in castor due to slow initial growth
upto 45 DAS, wider spacing (90 x 60 to 120 x 60 cm) and long duration
nature of the crop (150-180 days). The critical period of crop weed
competition for castor is 45-50 DAS.
In rainfed areas, 2 or 3 intercultures with the help of bullock drawn blade
harrows starting from 25-30 days after planting combined with one manual
intra-row weeding after first interculture effectively checks weed growth.
13
Alternately in areas of labour shortage, pre-emergence application of
pendimethalin 30% EC @ 1.0 kg a.i./ha (5.0 ml/l) followed by hand
weeding at 40 and 60 DAS recorded significantly higher seed yield.
In irrigated areas, integrated weed management schedule with pre-
emergence application of pendimethalin 30% EC @ 1.0 kg a.i./ha or
alachlor 50% EC @ 1.25 kg a.i./ha (5.0 ml/l) within 2-3 DAS in the
presence of soil moisture + post emergence application of Quizalofop-p-
ethyl @ 50 g a.i./ha (1.5-2.0 ml/l) + two times intercultivation + one hand
weeding has been recommended for effective and economical control of
weeds.
Of late, power weeders, tractor drawn blades and mini tractors efficiently
control the weeds.
Crop protection
Castor is attacked by a number of insect pests and diseases which impose a
great limitation in realizing the potential productivity. The magnitude of the
insect pests problem is quite high in Southern India where castor is grown
mainly as rainfed crop, while it is low in Gujarat and Rajasthan under late
kharif irrigated conditions. The important diseases and insect pests of castor
and their management methods are given in Table 2 and Table 3.
Selective mechanization
The impact of selective mechanization on critical operations like inter-
cultivation (power operated weeders) and sowing (tractor operated/bullock
drawn line marking and closing the furrow), harvesting and threshing (secateurs
and power operated threshers) on productivity of castor revealed significant
seed yield improvement by 14%, 10% and about 5%, at Hiriyur, Palem and
Yethapur respectively under rainfed conditions. Selective mechanization
reduced the labour requirement by 22-87 man days in different operations
resulting in reduction in cost of cultivation and higher economic returns across
locations.
14
Harvesting and Threshing
Castor, by nature is a multiple branching type. On an average, it produces
4 to 5 sequential order spikes over a span of 180 to 240 days, one each at
an interval of about 30 days. The main spike is ready for harvest within
90-120 days after planting. The subsequent pickings can be taken up at
intervals of 30 days.
Physiological maturity in castor is attained when some of the capsules in
a spike turn brown in colour. The matured spikes are cut and dried in sun
for a few days for easy threshing. For good seed filling, oil content and
germinability, harvest when the capsules turn yellow and start drying.
Threshing is usually done by either beating the capsules with sticks or
alternatively by trampling with bullocks or tractor wherever possible.
Power operated mechanical threshers are also available for the purpose.
Realizable yield potential
A well-managed kharif crop yields 1200-1500 kg/ha under rainfed conditions
while rabi crop yields about 2300-2500 kg/ha. Under irrigated conditions and
adoption of drip irrigation and fertigation can increase the seed yield upto
3500 kg/ha. Adoption of full package of practices including improved cultivar,
thinning, timely weed control, proper nutrition, need based irrigation and plant
health management will certainly improve the yields
Exploitation of perennial castor
The perennial nature of castor plant makes it ideally suited for planting on
field bunds, back yards, near farm houses, waste lands and on the banks of
rivers, irrigation canals etc. Varieties like CO-1 in Tamil Nadu, T-1, T-4 and
Kalpi-6 in Uttar Pradesh and Bihar are popular. In Tamil Nadu perennial castor/
local varieties are grown as boarder crop especially in cotton. All currently
recommended hybrids and varieties are also suited for such plantings. Adjust
spacing on the bunds so as to accommodate at least 20-50 plants/ha. The
perennial castor is grown in North-Eastern India primarily for rearing eri-silk
worm.
15
Value addition
Castor stubbles and shelled capsules can be incorporated in to the soil or can be
used in the preparation of vermicompost which in turn can be further applied
to agricultural fields to improve the soil fertility.
Castor leaves can be fed to eri silkworms (Ericulture) to produce 'eri silk'
which resembles cotton and is called as poor man's silk. It has been proved
to be an ideal subsidiary occupation to a large number of rural and tribal
populations as it provides ample scope for employment and income. Nearly
30% defoliation is permissible and the same can be used in ericulture without
foregoing economic yield. Besides, the farmers can be benefitted with an
additional income upto Rs. 6000 to 7500/ha through ericulture which is a boon
for rainfed castor growers.
Critical interventions for realizing higher yield
Always use quality seeds (certified hybrid/variety seed) procured from
authorized agencies.
Avoid continuous cropping of castor year after year in the same field,
particularly in the wilt endemic areas, and adopt at least 2 year crop
rotation.
Sow the crop at appropriate time with monsoon rains.
Treat the seed with recommended fungicides before sowing to ward off
wilt and root rot diseases.
Maintain optimum plant stand through adoption of recommended spacing
and seed rate.
Apply recommended fertilizer dose based on soil test values to ensure
better crop nutrition and yields.
Adopt recommended cultural practices.
During rabi adopt drip-fertigation for realizing higher yield and
economizing limited water.
Adopt need-based measures for managing diseases and insect pests.
Harvest the crop at appropriate time.

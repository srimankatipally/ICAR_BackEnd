INTRODUCTION
Linseed (Linum usitatissimum L.) is an important oilseed crop grown for
both seed and fibre. In South-west Asia including Turkistan, Afghanistan and
India, it is primarily grown for oil. In Asia Minor, South Russia and America, it
is traditionally cultivated for oil meant for edible as well as industrial purposes.
Almost every part of its plant is commercially utilized either direct or after
processing. It is next in importance to rapeseed-mustard grown during rabi
season. Presently, it is grown on 2.94 lakh hectares contributing 1.54 lakh tons
in production in the oilseed scenario of the country with a productivity level
of 525 kg/ha (Annual report of linseed 2017-18). Linseed occupies an important
position in India for its technical grade vegetable oil producing ability. Fibre
yielding property from the linseed stalk is an added advantage of this crop.
On small scale, the seed and its oil are directly used for human
consumption as flax seed breads, bagels and other baked and fried food stuffs
by a small segment of population. It is industrial oil and 80 per cent of oil is used
for paints, varnishes, a wide range of coating oils, linoleum, pad and printing
inks, leather and soap industries. Linseed is highly nutritious. It is a good
source of complete protein (all 8 essential amino acids), high order linolenic acid
(an essential polyunsaturated Omega-3 fatty acid), complex carbohydrates,
vitamins and minerals. Recent advances in medical research have found linseed
as best herbal source of Omega-3 and Omega-6 fatty acids which have immense
nutritional/medicinal effect on human body system. The oil cake is a good feed
for milch cattle. Linseed stem yields good quality fibre having strength and
durability. The fibre is lustrous and blends very well with wool, cotton and
silk, etc. Strong twines, canvas, suiting, shirting and various indispensable
products for defense purposes are manufactured from it. The woody matter
and short fibres may be used as raw pulp for making paper of quality. The
rough and strong linseed fibre can effectively be used for low-cost roofing
tiles based on convertible polymers and for fibre-reinforced plastics based on
unsaturated polyesters. The present productivity level of this crop (525 kg/ha)
can be increased more than three times, if it is grown under the following
recommended package of practices, reported from front line demonstration
(Annexure-1).
AREAS OF CULTIVATION
The major linseed growing states are Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh,
Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, Odisha, Jharkhand, Bihar and Rajasthan. In
addition to the above, it is also grown in Karnataka, Nagaland, Assam, West
Bengal, Jammu & Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh, Punjab and Meghalaya.
SEASON AND SOIL
Linseed is purely a rabi crop. It is often grown on marginal and sub-
marginal rainfed soils. It can be grown successfully on different soils ranging
from light soil to heavy one. If thrives well wet soil pH 5-7 but pH 6 is best.
Drought and high temperature (>32°C) during and after the flowering stage
reduce the yield. However, 20-30°C temperature is ideal.
VARIETIES
There are number of varieties available for cultivation in different cropping
systems, situations and for varied agro-climatic conditions in linseed. A total
of nine double purpose varieties were also developed which are having good
seed and fibre yielding potentials. There is an urgent need to replace the old
varieties from the farmers' field with new improved high yielding and disease
resistant varieties. Details about the varieties suitable for different regions and
situations are given in Table-1.
CROPPING SYSTEMS
Sole Cropping
Seed type of linseed can be grown either as sole crop or intercrop under
rainfed and irrigated conditions with as well as double purpose type (seed &
fibre producing) linseed can be grown under irrigated conditions. Double
purpose linseed can compete well with other crops in terms of net monetary
return, if it is grown as pure crop were recommended packages of practices
Though, linseed yields 20 q/ha seed and 12 q/ha fibre in experimental fields on
average basis, the national average is very low. There is a vast gap between
the achievable yield and the national average as has been demonstrated
through Frontline Demonstrations at farmer's field. In the light of its
commercial, medicinal and nutritional importance, everyeffort should be made
to use irrigation, fertilizers, plant protection measures, etc., to realize its
optimum yield for seed and fibre both.
Intercropping
Intercropping of linseed particularly with chickpea, lentil, durum wheat,
barley and safflower under rainfed condition and with wheat, potato, rapeseed
& mustard needs to be popularized on a priority basis. This will provide stability
to total production per unit land and may fetch higher net monetary returns.
Intercropping linseed with chickpea may reduce wilt and pod-borer damage in
chickpea. Growing linseed in intercropping system should be popularized in
place of mixed cropping. Some of the efficient intercrop combinations for various
states are indicated as follows:

Paira or Utera cropping
This is one of the best practices for utilizing the residual moisture in rice
fields where tillage is difficult. About 25% of the linseed area is under utera
cropping. In this practice, linseed is broadcasted in the standing paddy fields
at physiological maturity stage. Linseed is generally allowed to complete its
life cycle under the moisture stress, with inadequate nutrients and plant
protection measures, resulting in poor yields.
Tips to obtain higher yield in utera cultivation
RLC-143 (Utera Alsi) and RLC-153 (Utera Alsi 2) should be grown
in Chhattisgarh, Madhya Pradesh and adjoining areas of Odisha
whereas, Baner, Surabhi, LC-185 and Bhagsu in Himachal Pradesh.
Heavy-textured soils with good water-retention capacity should be
preferred.
Sufficient farm yard manure (FYM) or green manure should be
applied along with phosphatic fertilizers to paddy.
A dose of 10 to 20 kg N/ha should be applied 2 or 3 days before linseed
is sown.
The seed rate should be 35-40 kg/ha in M.P., Chhattisgarh,
Maharashtra, Odisha, and 50-60 kg/ha in Himachal Pradesh and
Balaghat area.
Cuscuta infestation, particularly in the Chhattisgarh, causes heavy
losses. To ensure clean cultivation, Cuscuta seeds should be
separated and discarded from the seed lot before sowing linseed.
Sowing linseed when rice is at the dough stage or physiological
maturity stage has proved to be the best. However, sowing may be done
10 to 12 days before rice is harvested, but should be completed by
October.
The weather should be clear at the time of sowing. Rains affect the
plant stand adversely.
Manual weeding should be done once or twice.
Crack system of sowing is a new method which can be followed in
areas where sufficient water is available. In this method, 5 cm deep
cracks are allowed to develop in the field when the paddy crop is at the
boot-leaf or panicle-initiation stage, and the field is watered
thereafter. After keeping the water standing for 5 to 7 days, the normal
practice of utera is followed. This method would give 60-100% more
yield and has been found to have no adverse effect on paddy yields.
Under utera system, linseed should be popularized as a mixed crop
with greengram, blackgram and lathyrus, which are under cultivation
in the system.
AGRONOMIC MANAGEMENT
Land preparation
The land should be ploughed 2 to 3 times followed by 2 to 3 harrowing
for fine tilth. To conserve moisture, it is advisable to create soil mulch with the
help of hoe after each good shower.
Fertilizer application
Linseed removes 60 kg N + 18.6 kg P₂O₅ + 54 kg K20 + 5.6 kg S+ 31 kg Calcium
+ 13 kg Mg + 46 g Zn + 664 g Fe + 177 g Mn + 13 g Cu per tonne of produce and
responds well to fertilizers. The recommended doses vary from state to state
(Table-2). Under rainfed condition, all the fertilizers should be applied at the
time of sowing whereas, under irrigated conditions, half dose of nitrogen and
full dose of phosphorus and potash is applied as a basal dose. The remaining
nitrogen is applied with the first irrigation i.e., about 35 days after sowing. An
application of 5t FYM/ha can save 25% of inorganic fertilizer. Seed inoculation
with N bio fertilizer + PSB and 5 t FYM/ha can save 25% fertilizer in double
crop system.
Sowing time
The sowing time varies widely (October to November) in different
states, depending upon the availability of soil moisture and water for irrigation
(Table-2). Early sowing helps the crop to escape attack of powdery mildew,
rust, linseed bud fly in different regions.
Spacing and seed rates
The spacing and seed rates recommended for different states are given in
Table-2.
Seeding depth
Depending upon the soil moisture, the seed should be placed 2-3 cm
below the soil. Shallow sowing in well prepared land having sufficient moisture
ensures good plant stand.
Seed treatment
Seed should be treated with Bavistin @ 1.5 g/kg seed or Thiram @ 3 g/kg
seed or Topsin-M @ 2.5 g/kg or Trichoderma viride or T. harzianum 4 gkg seed
to protect the crop from seed-borne diseases and to some extent from soil-borne
diseases also.
Irrigation
It is generally grown where annual precipitation ranges from 480-760 mm
and it response well to irrigation. Yield can be doubled if two irrigations are
given, the first at about 35 days after sowing and the second at about 65 days
after sowing. However, three irrigations (at about 35, 55 and 75 days after
sowing) have proved very effective in increasing the yield.
Weed control
Pre- emergence application of pendimethalin @ 1 kg/ha provide good
control of most of the weeds, whereas post emergence application of
clodinafop @ 60-80g/ha may manage weeds in the areas where isoproturon
is not working for the control of Phalaris minor. Application of metasulfuron
methyl @ 4g a.i./ha at 2-3 leaf stage of weed or post emergence for the
effective control of Medicago denticulta.
PLANT PROTECTION
More than two dozen insect-pests have been reported damaging linseed
crop right from seedling to harvest stage. Among these, bud fly (D. lini) is
designated as the key pest throughout the linseed growing areas, while leaf
miner, defoliators (semi-looper, lucerne caterpillar, Bihar hairy caterpillar)
and gram pod borer are considered as occasional or sporadic pests. However,
incidence of termite and cut worm may cause serious loss during seedling
stage of the crop especially under rain fed situation. Extent of yield losses in
linseed have been estimated ranging 7-26%, 19-97% and 33-98% by leaf miner,
bud fly and gram pod borer, respectively. Suitable crop stages coinciding with
favorable weather conditions trigger an epidemic of different insect-pests.
Average temperature between 16-20°C and relative humidity between 60-70%
found suitable for best multiplication of linseed bud fly. Egg, larval, pupal and
adult stages in one generation of bud fly complete in 2-5, 5-14, 5-11 and 1-3
days, respectively. One generation of this pest completes in 14-27 days and
there are four overlapping generation in one cropping season. A workable
management schedule for the insect-pests especially bud-fly is given in
Table 3.
There are a number of diseases to which linseed or flax is exposed but
Rust, Wilt, Alternaria blight and Powdery mildew are the major ones causing
losses up to 80-100%, 80%, 27-60% and 60% respectively. Rust is a serious
disease problem in Jammu and Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh, Punjab, Haryana
and hilly regions of Uttar Pradesh; Wilt is serious in semiarid regions of Central
India including Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh and Bihar; Alternaria
blight in Northern plains with high humidity i.e., Uttar Pradesh, Jharkhand,
Chhattisgarh and West Bengal; powdery mildew in Central and peninsular
regions viz., Madhya Pradesh, Odisha, Maharashtra, Rajasthan and Karnataka.
A phanerogamic plant parasite Cuscuta is a serious problem in Chhattisgarh
and adjoining parts of Odisha and Maharashtra. The packages for management
of diseases are illustrated in Table4.
HARVESTING
The crop should be harvested when the leaves are dry, the capsules have
turned brown and the seeds have become shiny.
STORAGE
Initial moisture and storage conditions are the most important factor
which affects the stability of linseed in storage. A relative humidity of 70%
with 8% moisture content in the seed is the best conditions for its storage.

TIPS TO OBTAIN HIGHER YIELD
Grow recommended resistant variety.
Use quality seed of the recommended varieties.
Treat the seed with fungicide, bactericide, Trichoderma, PSB,
Azotobactor etc., as recommended.
At the time of sowing appropriate soil moisture should be ascertained
in the field.
Take up sowing at appropriate time.
Keep the field weed free at least up to 40 days after sowing. Apply
recommended doses of fertilizers at appropriate time.
Adopt need based recommended plant protection measures against
insect pests and diseases.
Provide protective irrigation, if required.
Harvest the crop at physiological maturity.
FIBRE FROM DRY STALK OF LINSEED / FLAX
The double purpose linseed varieties are suitable for seed as well as fibre
production. A technical plant height (height from root to first branching) more
than 50 cm. is categorized for double purpose varieties. The double purpose
varieties are listed in Table-1. Fibre of linseed is one of the natural and
ecofriendly, fibre having more strength, fineness and durability than many
other fibres. It is soft, lustrous and flexible and has high water absorbency and
blends very well with wool, cotton, silk, etc. It is being used in the preparation
of strong twines, canvas, hosepipe, suiting-shirting and various indispensable
products for aero-space and aeronautical and defense purposes. Woody core
of stem and short fibre are used as raw pulp for making paper of quality. Fibre
from linseed plant and different products of linseed fibre are presented in Plate
3 and 4. Know how about obtaining the fibres from linseed's stalks are
illustrated in following steps.
1.
Harvest the crop from ground level.
2.
Make small bundles of the harvested crop, thresh out the seed and cut
the stalk from first branching to separate the important unbranched stalk
or cut the stalk from first branching and separate the seed portion and
stalk to each other thereafter thresh out for seed.
3.
Keep out the cut dry stalk separately for its retting.
4.
The retting of stalks can be done by different methods viz; water retting,
enzyme retting, chemical retting, dew retting, flash hydrolysis, gel retting,
but this can be done easily at farmers home/farm involving following
steps.
(a)
Place the stalk bundles in retting tank filled with water and leave it
for 2-3 days. If stalks are floating put some weight on it so that
stalks fully dip into the water.
(b)
Remove the stalks from tank and wash them about 8-10 times with
water thereafter allow it for sundry in open air.
5.
Now the stalks are ready for scutching (separating the fibre from retted
stalk). The scutching of fibre may be done with using either of following
procedures.
Manual
1.
Take small bundle of retted and dried stalk and beat it by hand mallet
(mungri) on plane and hard surface. Owing to this the wooden part of the
stalk will split out and resultant to this fibre can be obtained easily.
2.
This type of suctching can be done at small scale right at farmer's home.
Mechanical (Fibre scutching machine)
1.
Small bundle of retted and dried stalks placed on the feeder side of the
machine and allow to run the machine (manually or power operated). The
stalks get ruptured and come out from the delivery side.
2.
Shake the ruptured stalk by hand resulting to this fibre, which will remain
in hand and hard stick split out.
3.
If the rupturing of the stalk is not completed in one time, repeat the
process again to get the fibre completely free from the wooden part of the
stalk.
4.
Now, the power operated machines have been developed by which a
large quantity of stalks can be scutched in a short time.
Hacking
The scutched fibres are combed in order to remove short fibres and
extraneous matter. Hackling can be done by hand to remove short fibres, Trash
and knock out shove from scutched fibre.

Extraction of omega 3 fatty acid from linseed
Linseed oil is commonly used in food due to high percentage of omega
3 and omega 6 fatty acids. The fatty acid compositions of the oils were
determined by CHNS, GC-FID. GC/MSGC-FAME and ¹H NMR analyzer.
Among extraction methods, supercritical CO₂ is an environmentally friendly
process for selectively extracting the omega 3 and omega 6 fatty acids. The
extraction of linseed oil was carried out by using green solvent viz. supercritical
CO₂ fluid, soxhlet, mechanical screw press method, conventional and
accelerated method. Linseed oil is characterized by its low level of saturated and
high level of polyunsaturated fatty acids. The extracted oil is superior in quality
due to higher instauration/saturation ratio without solvent residue, so the oil is
most preferably used in food products.

RAPESEED-MUSTARD

Rapeseed-mustard (Brassica species) group of crops is the second most important oilseed crop after soybean. The genus Brassica comprises more than 30 species, mainly annual or biennial herbs, and many of them are similar in appearance. Among different Brassica species; B. juncea and B. rapa are highly polymorphic and the most widespread of the various Brassica species (Table 1).

In India, three different types of rapeseed-mustard crops are cultivated. These are brown mustard, Banarasi rai (rai or raya or laha)- B. juncea; sarson (yellow sarson, brown sarson), toria  and taramira or tara - Eruca sativa.  There are three different types of B. rapa, namely yellow sarson (B. rapa var. yellow sarson), brown sarson (B. rapa. var. brown sarson) and B. rapa. var. toria (toria). In trade, sarson, toria and taramira are known as rapeseed and rai as mustard.  Banarsi rai (B. nigra Koch) is a garden crop used as a spice. The cultivation of white mustard (Brassica alba syn. Sinapis alba) is no longer found in India. B. juncea, B. napus, B. carinata and yellow sarson are self-compatible and rest of the cruciferous oilseeds, viz. brown sarson, toria, taramira, Banarsi rai, and white mustard, are self-incompatible.

Indian mustard or brown mustard (rai) was originally introduced from China into north-eastern India, from where it had spread to Afghanistan via Punjab, eastern Afghanistan, together with the adjoining north-western India. It is predominantly cultivated in Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, Haryana, Madhya Pradesh and Gujarat. Its cultivation is also being extended to non-traditional areas of southern states like Karnataka, Tamil Nadu and Andhra Pradesh. The cultivation of brown sarson which once dominated the entire rapeseed-mustard growing region, now shadowed by Indian mustard. There are 2 different ecotypes of brown sarson: lotni (self-incompatible) and tora (self-compatible). The lotni is predominantly cultivated in colder regions of the country, particularly in Uttarakhand, Kashmir and Himachal valley. The tora on the other hand is cultivated in limited areas of eastern Uttar Pradesh. Yellow sarson is now mainly grown in Uttarakhand, Assam, Bihar, north-eastern states, Orissa, eastern Uttar Pradesh and West Bengal. Toria is a short-duration crop cultivated largely in Assam, Bihar, Orissa and West Bengal in the east mainly as winter crop. In Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Punjab, Uttarakhand and western Uttar Pradesh, it is grown as a catch crop.

Taramira is grown in the drier parts of north-west India including Rajasthan, Haryana and Uttar Pradesh. Taramira is relatively a recent introduction into India. It is believed to be a native of southern Europe and North Africa. Gobhi sarson and karan rai are the new emerging oilseed crops having limited areas of cultivation. Gobhi sarson is a long-duration crop confined to Haryana, Himachal Pradesh and Punjab.

Details of the recommended cultivation practices of rapeseed-mustard

Soil type and prevailing climatic conditions

The rapeseed and mustard crops are of the tropical as well as of temperate zones and require relatively cool temperatures for satisfactory growth. In India, they are grown in the rabi season from September-October to February-March. The rapeseed and mustard crops grow well in areas having 25 to 40 cm of rainfall. Sarson and taramira are preferred in low-rainfall areas, whereas Toria is grown in medium and high rainfall areas, respectively. Indian mustard requires comparatively higher temperature (30-32℃ maximum and 20-22℃ minimum) for germination and vegetative phase, and cool temperature during reproductive stage.

Rapeseed and mustard thrives best in well drained light sandy to clay loams soils of pH around 7-8.5, however, neutral pH is ideal for its growth and development. Raya may be grown on all types of soils, but toria does best in loam to clay loams. Sarson is suited to sandy loam soils and taramira is mostly grown on very light soils in parts of Rajasthan and Haryana. These crops cannot tolerate to water logging condition.

(II) Seed rate and time of sowing

Seed rate and spacing: Optimum plant population is a pre-requisite for a good harvest of the crop. For this, correct seed rate and spacing must be followed. In general, seed rate between 4 and 5 kg/ha is the optimum. Ideal spacing adopted in most of the states is 45 cm row to row and 15 cm plant to plant. Closer spacing of 30 cm × 15 cm is also adopted in some of the irrigated areas. Crop geometry 45 x 15 cm is suitable for hybrid mustard. Seed rate should be increased by 25% under rainfed and salt affected conditions.

Time of sowing: Timely sowing of the crop is very essential for realizing maximum yield. Further, the crop often also escapes insect-pests and diseases. The rapeseed- mustard crops are grown in diverse agroclimatic conditions, hence, optimum sowing dates vary widely. It should also be ensured that crop is sown when the maximum daytime temperature is not more than 32°C, which is essential for proper germination. The optimum sowing time for various crops in different states has been presented in Table 2.

Seed treatment: Seed treatment with pesticides, biofertilizers and drought alleviating microbes should be done to realize the high yield potential. Treat the seeds with Carbendazim @ 2g/kg or garlic bulb extract (2%) for protecting the crop from seed-borne diseases Sclerotina rot. Treat the seeds with biofertilizers; Azotobacter, PSB, and NPK consortia and drought alleviating microbes; CRIDA MI-I and MI-II before sowing/transplanting. Seed treatment with Imidaclorpid @ 6g/kg to protect the seedlings from early pest attack by painted bug.

Planting method adopted

Method of sowing: The crop is sown as per requirement like, flat sowing, raised bed and furrow sowing, zero-till sowing and transplanting. It is advisable to sow the crop in furrows with ridger-seeder. Seed soaked in water before sowing ensures good germination and early seedling vigour. The crop of rapeseed or mustard is sown at a depth of 4 to 5 cm in lines. Thinning is done three weeks after sowing to maintain the desired plant-plant distance.

System of Mustard Intensification

The System of Mustard Intensification (SMI) is a new cultivation method that involves planting mustard seedlings at a wider distance to provide space for growth and development of plants, and to increase the productivity per unit of time. SMI is also known as the System of Root Intensification (SRI).

Nursery preparation: A nursery of improved variety of mustard as recommended for the location should be raised before 15-20 days of date of transplanting with the help of root trainers or on raised soil beds. The nursery should be prepared in semi-shade conditions.

Field preparation: Field should be prepared as usually prepared for normal sowing. Small pits of 15-20 cm depth should be prepared at spacing of 45 x 15 cm for B. juncea, 60 x 60 cm for  B. carinata and 45 x 45 cm for B. napus. Recommended dose of fertilizers should be broadcasted in the field before 24 hours of transplanting

Transplanting method: Transplanting should be done in the evening hours to avoid any transplanting shock to young seedlings. 20 days old seedlings of 3-4 leaves should be transplanted. Care should be taken to transport the seedlings from nursery to field so that roots should not be exposed to air. After transplanting a light irrigation is to be given to establish the seedlings. Gap filling should be done after 6 days of transplanting.

Water application method and scheduling

Irrigation management: Normally, one irrigation at pre-flowering stage is adequate for toria and yellow sarson. In mustard, two irrigations are recommended in clay loam soils, first at rosette stage (30-35 DAS) and the second at the siliqua formation stage (65-70 DAS), and 4-5 irrigations in sandy to sandy loam soils. Furrow irrigation or flood irrigation methods are generally followed in India. To mitigate the terminal drought stress foliar spray of 1% KNO3 and 0.05% thiourea at 50% flowering and 50% siliqua filling stage should be done to increase seed yield. Soil application of hydrogel @ 2.5 kg/ha along with two foliar sprays of salicylic acid @ 200 ppm at flower initiation and siliquae formation stage is also recommended for higher yield of rapeseed-mustard under rainfed conditions. Water management

Oilseed brassicas are primarily rainfed or dryland crops. But with the availability of high-yielding varieties and remunerative prices, the cultivation of these crops has shifted to irrigated conditions. Presently, about 75% of the area under rapeseed-mustard is irrigated. Mustard requires about 300-400 mm of water and the consumptive use of water varies from 200 to 300 mm. Irrigations should be given at 0.6-0.8 IW/CPE ratio for maximum harvest of Indian mustard. In general, mustard crop requires 2 irrigations, first at branching stage (pre-bloom, 30 DAS) and the second at pod formation stage (pod filling, 60-65 DAS). In light soils, however, 5-6 irrigations gave better monetary returns.

Under rainfed conditions, moisture conservation is very important for getting higher yield. Mulches, Pusa Hydrogel (IARI product) and Jalshakti (a starch polymer and proprietary product developed by the National Chemical Laboratory, Pune), for conserving soil moisture under dryland agriculture conditions and anti-transpirants for reducing moisture loss from mustard plants have been tested and found useful. Seed treatment at 1.5% with Jalshakti accompanied with its soil application at 4-6 kg/ha resulted in 18-42% increase in seed yield of rapeseed-mustard at a number of centres. Soil application of hydrogel @ 2.5 kg/ha along with two foliar sprays of salicylic acid @ 200 ppm at flower initiation and siliquae formation stage is also recommended for obtaining sustainable higher yield of rapeseed-mustard under rainfed conditions.  Drought alleviating microbial formulation like, CRIDA MI-I and CRIDA MI-II should be used to increase the productivity and soil health.

Fertilizer usage and dosage applied

Nutrient management: Application of 40 kg N/ha is optimum for all rapeseed and mustard crops in rainfed areas. Recommended dose of fertilizer for rapeseed-mustard is presented in Table 4. In general, rapeseed-mustard crops are grown on coarse textured soils, which are very low in organic matter. Under such situations, organic manures can be used to boost the production of oilseeds and to improve fertilizer-use efficiency. Application of 10 tonnes FYM/ha was found effective in improving seed yield. Balanced NPK fertilization is all the more essential even at low levels of fertilizer usage for maintaining long-term fertility. Also adequate amounts of S, B and Zn need to be applied as per soil test analysis.

Rapeseed and mustard respond well both to organic and inorganic manures. Threshold level of organic carbon content 0.5% should be maintained to harvest good response to chemical fertilizers. Under irrigated conditions half of nitrogen and full dose of phosphorus and potash should be applied as basal dose at the time of sowing by placement method. The remaining half of the nitrogen should be applied at the time of first irrigation. If the crop is rainfed, use only half of the dose of recommended nutrient. Rapeseed and mustard have higher requirement for Sulphur, therefore, nitrogen should preferably be applied through ammonium sulphate and phosphorus from single super phosphate.

Nitrogen

Rapeseed-mustard group of crops respond well to nitrogen fertilization. Nitrogen deficiency may cause yellowness of lower leaves and stunted plant growth. A significant response has been reported to nitrogen application up to 60-80 kg N/ha under rainfed conditions and up to 120 kg N/ha under irrigated conditions. Higher N application may lead to succulent plant growth and more attack of insect pest and diseases. The nitrogen-accumulation curve during growth period is sigmoid in nature. The rate of nitrogen accumulation exceeds the rate of dry-matter production in early stages of growth up to 70 days after sowing and declines thereafter, resulting in shedding of leaves. So the time of N application in rapeseed-mustard is also important for getting better response to applied nitrogen. Split application of nitrogen 50% as basal and 50% as top-dressing after first irrigation gave better utilization of nitrogen and higher seed yield under irrigated conditions.

Phosphorus

This nutrient is deficient in about 50% of Indian soils. Phosphorus deficiency may cause purple leaves and pods, and stunted roots. Apart from genotype, soil type and soil-fertility status, level of applied nitrogen, potassium and environment for optimum crop growth are the factors that affect response to phosphorus. Single super phosphate is the best source of P as it contains 16% water soluble P, 12% sulphur and 21% calcium. SSP should be applied at the time of sowing, and below the seeds as basal application. However, its availability is highly dependent on pH, Ca, Fe and Zn.

Potassium

Potassium plays role in plant strength, and endurance to pest, diseases, frost and drought. Potassium deficiency may cause leaf margin burning, thin stem and poor plant growth. However, its deficiency may not be a serious problem for the rapeseed- mustard-growing areas. The response to K application had generally been low. The recommendation in different states varies from 20 to 40 kg K2O/ha and application as basal dose.

Sulphur

Sulphur is a vital component of S containing amino acids. Sulphur deficiency may cause yellowness of upper young leaves. To correct S deficiency in rapeseed-mustard in different soils, 30-40 kg S/ha is optimal under most field conditions. In intercropping system with potato, application of 30 kg S improved tuber and seed yield significantly over the control. Bantonite S (90% S) and elemental S (100% S) are the best sources of sulphur. These fertilizers should be applied as basal dose.

Micronutrients

Among several micronutrients, zinc deficiency is fairly widespread. Zinc deficiency may cause general bushiness in plants. Indian mustard responds to zinc application @ 5 kg/ha as basal or 0.5% spray. Zinc application increases internodes elongation and pollen viability in the plant. The best sources zinc is zinc sulphate (33% Zn and 15% S). Next to zinc, boron nutrition is important to mustard in some soils. Boron plays role in pollen fertilization and oil content. Deficiency of boron could be corrected either through soil application (1.0 kg/ha) or foliar application of borax (10.5% B). Besides S, Zn and B, calcium is also an important secondary nutrient which increases the cell wall strength. Calcium deficiency cause drooping of apical buds which ultimately dried up and there will be no seed setting. SSP contains 21% Ca so there is no need to apply extra calcium if we are applying SSP. All the secondary and micronutrients should be applied at the time of sowing as basal dose.

Application of bioregulators,  Biopower @ 25 kg/ha + 2 spary of Bioforce @ 2 ml/lit at 50% flowering and pod filling stage along with RDF increase seed yield by 14-20% and enhance fertilizer use efficiency in mustard.

Nitrogen economy through legumes grown in sequence

Legumes benefit the succeeding non-legume crops in terms of increased yields. In rapeseed-mustard, inclusion of leguminous crop in the sequence may help to economize N fertilizer requirement to the extent of 20-40 kg N/ha. Greengram-mustard and cluster Bean-Indian mustard are best legume sequence giving the maximum seed yield of mustard

Weed management

The most common weeds of rapeseed-mustard are Chenopodium album (bathua), Lathyrus spp. (chatrimatri), Melilotus indica (singe), Cirsium arvense (kateli), Cyperus rotundus (motha) and Fumaria parviflora (gajri). Invasion of Orobanche (broom rape) devastated its cultivation in Rajasthan. Initial 45-60 DAS are critical for crop-weed competition. Uncontrolled weeds in these crops may cause 20-70% reduction in yield. Weeding should be done soon after thinning. This, besides creating soil mulch and reducing moisture losses, helps in better growth and proper development of crop plants. Under rainfed conditions, one hand-weeding 25 DAS, while under irrigated conditions, 2 hand weeding 25 and 40 DAS are necessary for effective weed control. Pre-plant incorporation of Fluchloralin @ 1.00 kg a.i./ha or pre-emergence application of Pendimethalin @ 1.00 kg a.i./ha are quite effective in controlling weeds. If the weeds emerge after planting, Isoproturon @ 0.75 kg a.i./ha 30 DAS or quizalofop @ 50 g a.i./ha at 15-20 DAS should be applied. Intercropping of vegetables, spices and other crops with Indian mustard and Karan rai was found effective in reducing weed infestation as compared to their sole stand. Orobanche is a serious parasitic weed of rapeseed-mustard. The parasite appears as clumps of whitish, yellowish, brownish or purplish broom along with mustard stem at the ground level. It consists of a stout fleshy stem, 25-35 cm high and is covered by small thin and brown scaly leaves. For reducing infestation of Orobanche avoid continuous cropping of toria/mustard on the same field.

(VII) Thinning and inter-culturing

To keep an optimum plant population per unit area and uniform plant growth, thinning operation by removing the extra plants should be done at 15 - 25 days after sowing.  For providing good aeration to the roots, conservation of moisture and removal of weeds, one intercultural operation should be done with double wheel hand hoe before first irrigation, preferably along with thinning to save labour cost.

(IX). Adoption of IPM practices

Insect-pests

There are at least 38 insects associated with Brassica crops. Among them, mustard aphid is the key pest followed by white mustard fly, painted bug, pea leaf miner and Bihar hairy caterpillar (Table 7).

Diseases

Diseases cause average yield losses about 25.5%. Major diseases affecting the yield are Alternaria blight, white rust, downy mildew, powdery mildew, and Sclerotinia stem rot (Table 8).

Protection of crop from frost

Occasionally, frost prevails from last week of December to January end in north and north-western parts of the country resulting into considerable yield loss or sometimes total crop failure.

The following measures may be adopted to minimize the loss:

Irrigate the fields when the temperature islow.

Spray of Dimethyl sulphoxide (0.5 ml/litre) or Thiourea @ 1g/lit at the time of flowering and pod formation to prevent the effects of freezing onseeds.

Integrated disease management

Seed treatment (ST) with Trichoderma harzianum @ 10g/kg seed, soil application of Trichoderma (1 kg/ 50kg FYM), basal application of zinc sulphate @ 15 kg/ ha+ S (dose location specific) + boron (10 kg borax/ ha), line sowing 45x15 cm, no irrigation during 25th

December to 15th January minimize the disease incidence as compared to control, has been recommended as Integrated Disease Management (IDM) for zone II, III, IV.

Bio intensive IPM module for the management of mustard aphid:

Sow the crop at recommended time and avoid late sowing.

Avoid excessive use of nitrogenous fertilizers (urea) and apply only recommended dose.

At flowering, monitor the crop regularly at weekly intervals.

Apply control interventions only if aphid population increases economic threshold level.

Whenever, aphid population crosses economic threshold, first spray should be done with azadirachtin 3000 ppm @ 5ml/L water followed by second spray after 10 days.

For the chemical control of mustard aphid, spray the crop with thiamethoxam 25 WG @ 0.1 g/1 water (CIB recommended does) when the aphid population reaches economic threshold level. Precaution: Spray should be done in the afternoon when pollinators including honey bees are less active.

(X) Harvesting, Threshing and storage

Usually rapeseed-mustard crops are harvested as soon as 75% of pods turn yellow. Toria, which takes 75 to 90 days to mature, is the earliest crop to be harvested. The moisture in the seed should be around 30%. Harvesting can be done with the help of serrated sickles leaving about 15 cm high stubbles in the field. The mechanical reaper or harvester can also be used in large fields.

The crop may be tied in small bundles, stacked and dried in sun till the moisture content of the seed reaches between 12 and 20%. Threshing is done manually or by the usual method of treading by bullocks or by running a tractor over the dried plants spread on the threshing floor. When the produce is more, the combine or multicrop thresher is also used.

For safe storage, the moisture content in the seed should be 8%. Before storage, winnowing is done with the help of natural air current by slowly dropping the produce from a basket or by the use of power-operated cleaner. The seed, after drying in the sun is stored in gunny bags or bins.
